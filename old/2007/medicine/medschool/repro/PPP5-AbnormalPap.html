PPP Case 5: Abnormal Pap

Learning Objectives

  • Be familiar with the Bethesda System for reporting cervical cytology.
  • Recognize abnormal findings as reported by the Bethesda System.
  • Be aware of the management of patients with an abnormal Pap test result.
  • Describe the etiology of cervical cancer precursor lesions.
  • Be familiar with the therapeutic modalities used to manage patients with cervical cancer precursor lesions.

History

A 38-year-old G8P4Ab4 woman received notice that the resolut of her Pap test three weeks prior was abnormal. She had gone to a gynecologist at the insistence of a close friend. Her last Pap test had been eight years prior, at the time of her last pregnancy. She had undergone a postpartum tubal sterilization and had not seen a gynecologist since.

Her Pap test showed atypical squamous cells of undetermined significance (ASC-US).

Past Medical History

  • Smoker since age 13 (1 PPD)
  • First full term pregnancy delivery age 18
  • Gestational diabetes during her 3rd pregnancy and with each succeeding pregnancy
  • Coitarche age 15 with ~12 lifetime sexual partners
  • No history of alcohol or drug abuse or sexually transmitted diseases

Past Surgical History

  • Tonsillectomy and adenoidectomy age 4
  • Appendectomy age 23
  • Tubal ligation age 30

Pertinent Physical Examination Findings

Completely normal, including well-healed infraumbilical and right lower quadrant abdominal scars

Discussion Points/Questions

What possible disease entities could be responsible for this patient's abnormality on pap?

Minor cervical cell changes found during a Pap test may be caused by:

  • Infection, including infection with the human papillomavirus (HPV)
  • Inflammation of cervical cells
  • “precancerous” changes
  • Idiopathic, or unknown changes
How would you proceed with the evaluation/management of this patient's abnormality?

ASC-US abnormalities may be followed with “watchful waiting”. (But then, really, what can't?) An HPV test, either on the available Pap test or on a new sample, may be warranted to assess any additional risk of cervical cancer. The NIH Consensus statement1 suggests that the patient in question must undergo thorough diagnostic evaluation, specifically:

Ideally, diagnostic evaluation should include colposcopic examination and biopsies of representative areas of the cervix usually including an endocervical curettage (scraping of the interior wall of the cervix). Diagnostic conization of the cervix (removal of a cone of tissue) may also be required if the location of the lesion makes colposcopy unsatisfactory, if the diagnosis from an endocervical curettage so indicates, or if the cytologic and histologic findings conflict.

Is there more than one acceptable management? Which one would you pick? Why?

The above two choices (“watchful waiting” versus “thorough evaluation”) represent two extremes. It may (or may not) be beneficial to discover neoplasia in a very early stage, but this must be weighed against the risks of minor surgery (as in cervical biopsy), patient discomfort and anxiety, and the continued chance of false positives in these tests.

It is almost certainly safe to perform only the minimally-invasive colposcopy (that is, “minimally-invasive” relative to biopsy, curettage, or conization). Discovery of grossly abnormal areas suggests the need for biopsy, whereas lack of gross changes is less likely to be associated with higher-grade cervical intraepithelial neoplasias. Assuming a lack of gross abnormalities, it is likely to be safe to repeat the Pap smear in six months to one year.

Is this patient at increased risk for developing cervical cancer? What would be this risk? What is the etiology of cervical cancer precursor lesions?

More than 60% of ASC-US changes go away on their own. 5-10% of all Pap tests result in a diagnosis of ASC-US. Approximately 10-15% of women with a cytologic result of ASC-US will have biopsy-confirmed CIN-2 or CIN-3, which is more likely to progress to cervical carcinoma or persist as CIN than to regress. Still, only 22% of CIN-2 progresses (to either invasive cervical cancer or carcinoma in situ/CIN-3), and 14% of CIN-3 progresses. In all, the majority of invasive cancers occur now in women who have not been screened at all, rather than in those screened too infrequently.

That, of course, refers to the general population. This patient's risk may be somewhat higher, due to her history of smoking, multiple partners, and multiple births. Specific risk factors for CIN include sexual activity at an early age, history of sexually transmitted infections, multiple sexual partners, or engaging in sexual activity with promiscuous men. These data firmly implicate a venereally transmitted agent as a major causal factor: this agent is infection with the human papillomavirus (HPV). In fact, the association between HPV and cervical neoplasia has proven so strong that most other behavioral, sexual, and socioeconomic covariables are found to be dependent upon HPV infection and do not hold up as independent risk factors. The association of some types of HPV infection and the development of CIN and/or cervical cancer has been consistently supported by studies such that the infection is considered necessary (though not sufficient) for development of cervical cancer. The mechanism by which certain subtypes of HPV cause CIN is unclear.

Other consistently reported risk factors include cigarette smoking, muliparity, and immunodeficiency. Some weak risk association has been found in oral contraceptive use and certain dietary deficiencies.
